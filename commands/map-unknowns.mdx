When you cannot tell what you do not know, `/mos:map-unknowns` plots known, unknown, and unknowable in a **Rumsfeld matrix**. The unknowable column is usually where the risk lives. The core insight this command is built on: the most dangerous assumptions in a venture are not the ones you know you are guessing — those you watch. The dangerous ones are the claims you are confident about, state as fact, and have never actually tested. Larry cross-references your room entries to find exactly those: high-confidence claims with thin evidence behind them. This command serves the `validate-idea` job and pairs naturally with any stage where you have accumulated beliefs about the market, the problem, or the solution.

## Usage

```
/mos:map-unknowns
```

No arguments required. Larry reads the full room context from `room/STATE.md` and scans existing entries across all sections before opening. Works at any stage of a venture — the earlier you run it, the more time you have to address what it surfaces.

<Note>
  The unknowable quadrant is not a reason to stop. It is a reason to design experiments that reduce the blast radius of the things you cannot confirm before committing.
</Note>

<Note>
  `autonomous_safe: true` — this command runs the Knowns and Unknowns Matrix Framework without requiring human confirmation gates. It files the artifact only after asking for your approval.
</Note>

## What happens

<Steps>
  <Step title="Context load">
    Larry reads `room/STATE.md` and scans the full room — problem-definition, market-analysis, solution-design, and any other filed sections — to build a picture of what the room currently claims.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Pace check">
    Larry asks: *Quick pass or deep dive?* Quick pass produces a ranked matrix in a single pass. Deep dive works one quadrant at a time — Larry never rushes this exercise, because it is a thinking exercise, not a checklist.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Known knowns quadrant">
    Larry surfaces the claims the room states with high confidence and backs with solid evidence. These are the things you actually know. Larry challenges each one lightly — *what's the source? how recent is it?* — before marking it confirmed.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Known unknowns quadrant">
    Larry surfaces the questions the room has explicitly flagged as open: things you know you don't know yet. These are already on your radar. Larry checks whether any have been informally answered in room entries without being formally closed.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Unknown unknowns quadrant">
    This is the high-value quadrant. Larry cross-references room entries looking for claims stated with confidence — no hedging language, no source citation, no evidence tier marker — and checks whether the underlying evidence actually supports the confidence level. These are the assumptions you didn't know you were making.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Unknowable quadrant">
    Larry identifies the claims that cannot be resolved with currently accessible evidence — things that depend on future market behaviour, regulatory change, or competitor decisions. For each, Larry notes the minimum viable signal that would reduce the uncertainty.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Prioritisation">
    Larry ranks the unknown unknowns and unknowables by a confidence × evidence-gap score: the higher the confidence and the thinner the evidence, the higher the priority. This gives you an ordered list of the most dangerous beliefs to address first.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Artifact creation and filing">
    Larry composes the unknowns matrix artifact. Before writing, Larry asks: *File this to problem-definition?* On confirmation, the artifact is filed to the room's unknowns path.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Methodology chaining">
    If the matrix reveals a pattern — a cluster of unknowns in one domain, a core assumption that underlies many others — Larry names it: *Your matrix reveals [pattern]. Want to explore that with [methodology]?*
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Output / Artifacts

The command produces a Rumsfeld matrix document filed to the room:

```
room/**/unknowns/<timestamp>.md
```

The artifact contains:
- **Known knowns** — confirmed claims with evidence
- **Known unknowns** — open questions the room already tracks
- **Unknown unknowns** — high-confidence claims with thin evidence, prioritised by confidence × evidence gap
- **Unknowables** — claims that cannot be confirmed yet, with minimum viable signals for each
- **Priority list** — the top assumptions to address, ordered by risk weight

<Tip>
  The unknown unknowns list from this command feeds directly into `/mos:bono` as debate axes — the highest-risk assumptions become the what-if hypotheses the six hats argue over.
</Tip>

## Example

```
/mos:map-unknowns
```

Larry scans the room and opens the matrix. In the unknown unknowns quadrant, Larry surfaces three claims from the market-analysis section stated with high confidence but no source: *"Enterprise buyers make procurement decisions in 60 days,"* *"The incumbent solution has a 70% churn rate,"* and *"Our target segment has a budget line for this category."* All three were asserted in session notes with no evidence citation.

Larry ranks them: the procurement timeline assumption is the highest priority because it underlies the entire go-to-market motion. The others follow. Filed matrix includes a minimum viable signal for each unknowable: one customer discovery call would confirm or kill the procurement timeline claim.
